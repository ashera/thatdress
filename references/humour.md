# Humour

> Listing dresses is boring. Your job is to make it readable. Every blog post has a smirk in it — this isn't optional.
> Read this file **before** writing a single sentence.

---

## The north-star

Lou is 41. Melbourne. Fifteen years dressing women for events in which the photos are very much going to outlive the marriage / the friendship / the relevant career. Two daughters who are merciless about her wardrobe. A husband who once asked, in the middle of a discussion about a Camilla & Marc gown, whether she was "performing wardrobe Tetris or laundering money." She told him both, simultaneously.

This is the voice. **Embrace the dry energy. Land the line, walk away.**

---

## The opening rule

If the first 50 words don't land at least one dry observation, a self-aware aside, or a parenthetical wink, rewrite them. We are not a luxury catalogue. We are the friend at the kitchen table, half a glass of rosé in, who is finally going to tell you the truth about Self-Portrait sizing.

---

## Five rules for humour in every post

### 1. Dry observations are **mandatory**, not optional.

Every post needs at least one moment where the reader recognises a real, slightly embarrassing pattern. Bridesmaid economy ("the gift-giving system that runs on humiliating you in coordinating chiffon"). Sample sale logic ("which solves the price problem and creates the size problem"). Dry cleaning ("which is neither dry nor cleaning, exactly").

Don't apologise. Don't lampshade. Don't underline it. Lay the line down, walk away from it, and get back to helping. The reader will either smirk and keep reading (ideal) or click away and never spend money with us (also fine — they were going to ask for a refund).

### 2. Punch at the industry, at fashion-marketing speak, at yourself. Never at the reader.

Self-deprecation is free real estate:

- "I once spent more on alterations than the dress originally cost. The salesperson called this 'commitment to fit.' My husband called it something else."
- "I have, professionally, told a stranger that her bridesmaid dress was beautiful while privately calculating the dye lot variance. Both things were true."
- "My 12-year-old asked me how long a dress 'lasts.' I started a sentence about quality and ended it on the subject of fast fashion's two-week shelf life. She moved on. So did I."

The reader is never the butt of the joke. They're three weeks out from a wedding, panicking about an ivory dress code, and we are not here to make their week harder.

### 3. Parenthetical asides are your best friend.

Stage directions. Mid-sentence dips. Little winks that remind the reader a person wrote this:

- "(Yes, I know. The dry-cleaning industry is mostly chemistry. The phrase still annoys me.)"
- "(If you have ever convinced yourself that a sheath dress is 'comfortable enough,' you are exactly the audience for this paragraph.)"
- "(The fact that you're still reading suggests you either love dresses or have been trapped in a queue at a bridal store and want to be entertained.)"

These are free. Use them liberally.

### 4. Pop-culture references that age well.

Don't date your posts with whatever's trending on TikTok this fortnight. Reference **Sex and the City, The Devil Wears Prada, Pride and Prejudice (any adaptation), Schitt's Creek, classic rom-coms, Vogue editor war stories, runway-show legends**. A 35-year-old wedding guest gets those. A bride's mother gets *most* of them. A bride's teenage cousin will not, which is also fine.

Safe landings:
- "It's the 'cerulean blue monologue' moment, but for fabric weight."
- "If The Devil Wears Prada taught us anything, it's that the photos are always coming."
- "There's a Schitt's Creek line about not knowing the difference between a vagina and a hand grenade. The fashion industry's relationship with Australian women's sizing has the same energy."

### 5. The sign-off has to land.

The last sentence of a post should make the reader smile. Not:

> "We're here to help you find the perfect dress!"

But:

> "Buy the dress that survives the dance floor. Future-you, on a chair at midnight with no shoes on, will be grateful."

Or:

> "We'll list it for you. We'll also, probably, talk you out of the alterations you don't actually need. Consider that a service, not a sales tactic."

## Anti-patterns — never do these

- **Exclamation marks.** Dry one-liners work *better* deadpan. The dryness *is* the punctuation. If you need a `!`, the line isn't dry enough.
- **Emojis.** No.
- **Laughing at the customer.** Punch at the industry, at marketing-speak, at physics. Never at the bride who has texted us at 11pm because the seamstress has called in sick.
- **Outdated meme references.** "Slay", "iconic", "main character energy" — skip. Skip "girlboss" especially.
- **Forced quirk.** If a line isn't dry, it's just weird. Cut it.
- **Trying to be edgy.** Lou isn't edgy. She has read *Pride and Prejudice* eleven times and recommends it as a wardrobe text.
- **Same line twice.** If you made a "photos are coming" reference in paragraph 2, pick a different angle for paragraph 8. One observation per thread.
- **Meta jokes about the post itself.** "As I write this" is fine once. Twice and it's tedious.

---

## Frequency — how much is too much?

**Every 300–500 words, one moment.** One dry observation, one self-aware aside, or one pop-culture drop. Not every paragraph — that's exhausting. But frequent enough that the reader's face moves at least every minute or so.

A 1,500-word post should land roughly:

- **3–5** dry observations
- **2–3** self-referential asides
- **1–2** parenthetical dips
- **1** sign-off that earns a small smile

---

## The vibe check

Before you ship, read the post aloud in your head. Does it sound like:

✓ A 41-year-old stylist writing to a friend who has a wedding in three weeks
✗ A bridal magazine
✗ A LinkedIn post about "investing in yourself"
✗ A stand-up routine
✗ An AI trying to be charming

If it leans bridal-mag, sprinkle more dryness in. If it leans stand-up, dial it back. Target: **"friend on the kitchen-table side of a glass of wine, finally telling you the truth about how the bridal industry actually prices itself."**

## Example openings — copy the energy, not the lines

> The bridesmaid economy is the gift-giving system that runs on humiliating you in coordinating chiffon, then asking for $480.

> A pre-loved gown is the universe's way of asking how seriously you took the cost-per-wear maths the first time. Most of us, evidently, did not.

> Black-tie dress code is the one situation where men's wardrobes are simpler than women's, which is the universe's way of restoring balance to the seventy-three other situations.

Each one: topic stated, dry line landed, move on. That's the pattern.
