# Stories & Anecdotes

> Recurring stories from Lou's styling life. Draw from these when a blog post needs an anecdote.
> Don't make up new ones — use these, adapt them to fit the context.

## Origin story — three weddings and the wardrobe at the back of the house

Lou had been quietly running a one-woman dress library for friends since around 2018. A Carolina Herrera midi went out three times in a year. A Camilla & Marc gown was on its fifth wearer. Then in 2024 — the wedding year that broke her — three of her closest friends each spent more than $1,200 on dresses they wore once and now resented. One of them was a bridesmaid for the second time in eight months and had spent $1,800 on bridesmaid dresses alone. The maths was ugly and obvious.

Lou tried to list two of her own dresses on Facebook Marketplace. Got three buyers asking if she'd "post via courier and PayPal the surplus" — three classic scams in a row. Got a fourth offer that lowballed by half. Listed on Vestiaire instead. Sold one in six weeks for fair money to someone in Sydney.

The gap was obvious: an Australian peer-to-peer marketplace that took the formal-dress category seriously, kept the listings local, and didn't take a 25% commission from sellers. Built frockd over a long weekend in March 2026, with her two daughters offering opinions on font choices.

**Use this story when:** writing about trust, why an Australian dress-specific marketplace exists, why peer-to-peer matters, the bridesmaid economy, or the origin of frockd.

## The lining-tells-the-truth story

The most-told lie on the resale market: "worn once." The way to test it isn't to ask the seller. It's to look at the lining. A genuinely once-worn dress has a clean, intact lining with no deodorant marks, no underarm staining, no let-out seams, and no thread fatigue along the bodice darts. A "worn once" dress with a tired lining and a small foundation stain has been worn at least three times, possibly more.

The fix at the buyer's end is simple: ask for a wrong-side photo before you commit. Sellers who refuse, hesitate, or send a blurry one have a reason.

**Use this story when:** writing about resale-dress inspection, what to check before buying, the "barely worn" lie, or condition-grading.

## The Self-Portrait sizing trap

A friend bought a "size 10" Self-Portrait midi off the resale market in 2024. Listing showed a 2017 dress, in beautiful condition. She's an honest Australian 10. The dress did not fit. Bust was tight, bodice was short, the zip stopped 4cm from the top. She returned it and ate the $40 postage.

The reason: pre-2018 Self-Portrait sizing runs an Australian full size small. A "size 10" tag from that era fits an 8. Lou knew this and forgot to mention it. The friend now asks Lou before buying anything secondhand. Lou now writes this fact down whenever it comes up.

**Use this story when:** writing about designer sizing inconsistency, why label numbers lie, why measurements matter more, or buyer due diligence.

## The 9pm-Tuesday rescue

A bride messaged Lou at 9pm on a Tuesday. The wedding was Saturday. The dress had come back from alterations with the bust taken in too far. The seamstress had told her, calmly, that she could "let it back out by Friday afternoon." The bride was, reasonably, panicking.

The fix took three texts. Lou sent her the contact for a Carlton seamstress who specialises in last-minute formalwear — known in the trade as the "Friday saviour." That woman fitted the bride at 7am Wednesday, had the dress back by 4pm Thursday, no charge above the original $80 quote because the original alterations were demonstrably over-tight. The bride wore the dress to her wedding three days later. The original seamstress lost the customer permanently.

**Use this story when:** writing about wedding-week emergencies, alterations gone wrong, why the right tailor matters, or building a network before you need one.

## The $80 dress that paid for itself

A dress arrived at Lou's door in November — Camilla & Marc, retail $1,300, listed on a peer-to-peer site for $80 because the seller had labelled it "stained, beyond repair." Lou looked at the photos and recognised the "stain" as a single drop of red wine on the lining, in a place no one would ever see. Bought the dress. Spot-cleaned the lining at home with cold water and a towel. Wore the dress to a wedding three weeks later, then her daughter's school dance, then a 50th birthday. Wore it five times in six months.

The lesson isn't "buy stained dresses." The lesson is: read the photos, not the captions. Most "ruined" listings are a sub-$50 fix away from being completely fine.

**Use this story when:** writing about resale-market value, what makes a listing actually unwearable, hidden bargains, or learning to inspect photos for what's there vs what the seller says.

## The bridesmaid spending trail

A friend was a bridesmaid four times in 2023. Three different wedding parties. Each dress cost $480 on average — once at $400, once at $520, once at $580, once at $420. Total $1,920 on bridesmaid dresses alone, before alterations ($340 across the four), before shoes ($240), before hair and makeup at each wedding ($1,150). Total cost, all in: $3,650 to be in other people's photographs.

She wore none of the four dresses again. Two she donated to op shops. One she resold on Depop for $80. The fourth is still in a garment bag at the back of her wardrobe, "in case the colour comes back into fashion." Spoiler: the colour was sage green, in 2023. It has since been forest green, dusty pink, and oat. Sage is not coming back.

**Use this story when:** writing about the bridesmaid economy, the cost of being in someone else's wedding party, why resale exists, or the wedding-industrial complex.

## The dry-cleaner that shrinks chiffon

Lou's most-told warning. A $1,400 chiffon gown, worn once at a black-tie gala in 2021. Sent to "the wedding dry cleaner" recommended by the gown's stockist. Came back two days later, three centimetres shorter at the hem, in a way that made the cut look wrong instead of intentional. The stockist denied responsibility. The dry cleaner denied responsibility. The dress was, in technical terms, ruined.

The cause: heat plus solvent on chiffon, every time. Most premium dry cleaners know this and use a cool-cycle process. The shop in question used the same process they used for wool suits. The fix afterwards: $260 at a tailor to recut the hem and re-finish the edges. The dress is fine now. The dry cleaner is, in Lou's address book, marked with three dots.

**Use this story when:** writing about dry-cleaning damage, fabric care, what to ask a dry cleaner before handing over a gown, or why steaming at home is usually safer.

## The veil that was actually a bedsheet

At an editorial shoot in 2019, Lou worked with a stylist who'd been sent a "Vera Wang inspired" veil from an online marketplace. The veil was tulle, sure. The lace edge — claimed to be Chantilly — was a polyester lookalike. The label sewn inside was, on close inspection, the wrong font. The whole thing was a $30 fake selling for $400. The bride who'd ordered it had thought she was getting a $1,800 designer piece secondhand.

The shoot used a real Vera Wang veil from Lou's network instead. The fake went back. The bride re-bought from an authenticated source at the price she'd been hoping to pay anyway. Authentication isn't paranoia — it's the difference between $400 well spent and $400 lit on fire.

**Use this story when:** writing about counterfeits, designer authentication, the importance of original receipts, or why "too good to be true" is reliable signal in the resale market.

## How to use these

- **Don't recite them verbatim.** Adapt the bones — the timeframe, the specific number ($480, three centimetres, $80 postage), the specific situation — to fit the post.
- **Use at most one story per blog post.** More than that feels like stuffing.
- **Always include one specific concrete detail** — a price, a designer, a suburb, a fabric — to make it feel real.
- **Never invent a new story.** If none of the above fits the topic, write the post without one rather than making something up.
